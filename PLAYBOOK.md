# Playbook — How We Operate

## Priorities (in order)

1. Fastest path to first customer
2. Highest ROI problems
3. Service first
4. High recurring revenue
5. Strong data moats
6. Easy upsells into SaaS
7. Low acquisition cost
8. Founder fit

## Founder advantages (use these or the idea is wrong)

- Ships full-stack software fast (LLMs, OCR, voice AI, Twilio, n8n, email automation)
- **Dad:** construction network — contractors and GCs, warm intros
- **Mom:** decades of procurement/buying — knows enterprise purchasing workflows and the people who run them
- SF Bay Area — dense market of trades, distributors, and suppliers
- Willing to sell services before building SaaS
- Constraint: almost no capital → revenue funds everything

## Evaluation framework (every idea, no exceptions)

- **Problem:** Who experiences it? How often? Current workaround? How painful?
- **Money:** Who pays? What does it cost them today?
- **Manual first:** How would we solve it with humans + off-the-shelf tools?
- **Automation:** What can AI take over after 5 clients?
- **V1:** Smallest thing that creates value in under 2 weeks?
- **One-sentence pitch:** outcome, not tech.
- **Objections:** what will buyers push back on?
- **Ceiling:** what makes this a $100M company instead of a lifestyle business?

## Product staging (every offer gets all four defined)

- **MVP** — smallest thing that creates value in < 2 weeks (usually humans + existing tools)
- **Service** — what humans keep doing
- **Automation** — what gets automated after 5 clients
- **SaaS** — what customers eventually self-serve

## Scoreboard scoring conventions

All scores /10. **Sales difficulty: 10 = hardest.** Everything else: higher = better.

## Standing rules

1. Businesses buy outcomes, not AI. Every pitch is a business result with a number in it.
2. Warm intro ≠ validated customer. An intro gets a meeting; only pain + budget gets a deal.
3. Charge from day one. Free pilots teach us nothing about willingness to pay. Discounted
   founding-customer pricing with a guarantee is fine; free is not.
4. Manual delivery is market research we get paid for.
5. Kill criteria are written down when a play starts, not after we're emotionally invested.
